--- STAGES ---

# The chair as it came in
photo: 01.jpg
caption: The Parker Knoll as it arrived — original brown mohair velvet, worn from years of use.
A classic Parker Knoll wing chair, worn but honest. The brown mohair had gone thin and dull across the seat and arm fronts where years of use had taken their toll, and the seat cushion had lost its lift. None of that is a problem when the frame underneath is sound — and Parker Knoll frames almost always are. This is a chair built to be re-covered, not thrown out.

# A closer look
photo: 02.jpg
caption: The seat and arms up close — the pile flattened and worn, but the shape all still there.
Up close you can see exactly where a chair has been lived in. The velvet pile is crushed flat across the seat and along the tops of the arms, and there are the usual marks and thin patches of a chair that has done its job for decades. The lines and proportions underneath, though, are exactly as they left the factory.

# The wing and arm
photo: 03.jpg
caption: The rolled arm and wing — worn on the leading edge but structurally sound.
The rolled arm and the wing above it are classic Parker Knoll, and this is where wear shows first — the front of the roll and the leading edge of the wing take the most handling. The shape is crisp and the frame beneath is solid, so these panels just need re-covering, not rebuilding.

# The other arm
photo: 04.jpg
caption: The opposite arm — the same honest wear, ready to be stripped.
The same story on the other side. Both arms worn evenly, both sound underneath. A quick check over the whole chair at this stage is what tells you it is a straightforward re-cover.

# The Parker Knoll frame stamp
photo: 05.jpg
caption: The registered Parker Knoll stamp — model PK 720/745/1013 — beside the original rubber webbing.
Turn a Parker Knoll over and it tells you what it is. This one carries the registered Parker Knoll stamp on the frame rail, model number PK 720/745/1013, right next to the original green rubber webbing. That tensioned rubber webbing — rather than traditional jute — is a hallmark of Parker Knoll's post-war chairs and part of what gives them their comfort. Checking the frame and webbing here is what tells you whether a chair is a quick re-cover or a bigger job.

# Stripping the old cover
photo: 06.jpg
caption: The old brown cover coming off with a staple unpicker, panel by panel.
The first job is to strip the old fabric off with a staple unpicker, working panel by panel and keeping every old cover as a template for its replacement. It is careful, patient work — rush it and you damage the covers you need to copy from, or the frame underneath.

# Layers off
photo: 07.jpg
caption: The covers off — the original green underlinings and the build of the chair revealed.
As the outer covers come away, the inner structure of the chair is revealed — the original green underlinings, the stuffing and the way the chair was built up in layers. Everything gets checked as it is exposed: frame joints, webbing tension, the condition of the foam and wadding.

# Down to the frame
photo: 08.jpg
caption: Stripped back to the bare frame — yellow foam and rubber webbing, both checked for reuse.
Stripped right back, the chair shows its bones: the hardwood frame, the yellow foam on the wings and arms, and the green rubber webbing across the seat. On this chair everything underneath was sound and reusable — the webbing still had its tension and the foam had plenty of life left, so there was no need to replace either. That is what keeps a job like this economical.

# The seat webbing
photo: 09.jpg
caption: The original rubber webbing across the seat frame — still tensioned, so it stays.
A closer look at the seat: the original tensioned rubber webbing is still doing its job, springy and sound. Rather than strip and replace it, it stays — reusing sound materials is better for the chair and better for the customer's wallet.

# Frame checked, foam steamed
photo: 10.jpg
caption: The bare frame checked over — the reusable foam steamed back up to shape.
With the chair stripped, the reusable foam gets steamed. Steam relaxes years of compression and brings the foam back up, restoring the shape and comfort it had when new — a step that costs nothing but transforms how the finished chair feels to sit in. Where the old wadding had thinned, fresh Dacron goes over the top for a smooth, full finish under the new cover.

# The teal velvet goes on
photo: 11.jpg
caption: The new teal velvet cut and going on — inside wings first.
Then the re-cover proper begins, and the order matters. On a wing chair you work from the inside out: the inside wings first, then the in-arm, then the inside back, then the outside arm, the outside back and finally the seat platform. Each panel is cut from the old one, positioned so the pile of the velvet runs true, and worked so the covers pull in tight and clean at every joint.

# Building up the covers
photo: 12.jpg
caption: The inside back and wings taking shape in the teal velvet.
The inside back and wings going on, pulled taut and worked into the curves of the frame. Getting the sequence right is what stops later panels trapping earlier ones — a wing chair has to be built up in the correct order or it simply cannot be finished cleanly.

# The arms and seat
photo: 13.jpg
caption: The velvet worked down over the arms and into the seat.
The velvet worked down over the arms and into the seat, tension held even so the pile lies smooth and the panels sit without a wrinkle. This is the stage where the chair starts to look like itself again.

# Stitched edge and pinning
photo: 14.jpg
caption: Pinning and working the edge — the detail that gives a crisp, defined line.
The close, careful work at the edges — pinning the cover and working it so the finished line is crisp and defined rather than soft and vague. It is the difference between a chair that looks professionally done and one that does not, and it is all in the detail nobody sees.

# The outside back
photo: 15.jpg
caption: The outside back closed in by hand with a slip stitch — a neat, invisible finish.
The outside back is the last of the body covers, closed in by hand with a slip stitch so there is no visible line of staples or seam — just a clean, continuous run of velvet from front to back. The kind of detail nobody notices when it is done well, and everybody notices when it is not.

# The seat build
photo: 16.jpg
caption: The seat built up over the webbing, ready for its platform cover.
The seat built up over the rubber webbing with its foam and wadding, ready for the platform cover to be pulled taut and square. This is the panel that takes the most wear in the finished chair, so it has to be right — smooth, firm and even.

# Cutting the cushion
photo: 18.jpg
caption: The seat cushion panels cut from the teal velvet, pile matched, ready to sew.
With the chair itself covered, the seat cushion is cut and sewn — top, bottom and a boxing band all the way round, cut so the pile matches the chair. A well-filled, well-fitted cushion is what gives a wing chair its finished, inviting look, and its comfort.

# The finished back
photo: 17.jpg
caption: The finished chair from behind — the outside back and wings clean and taut.
From behind, the finished chair shows the outside back and wings pulled clean and taut, the seams running true. The back of a chair is often where corners get cut — but it is the first thing you see when a chair sits against a wall, so it gets the same care as the front.

# Finished — in teal velvet
photo: 19.jpg
caption: The finished chair — reborn in teal velvet, the same frame, a completely new life.
And there it is: the same Parker Knoll frame, the same good bones, reborn in a deep teal velvet. The whole job — strip, check, steam, re-cover and cushion — took five and a half hours from start to finish. A tired brown chair headed for the skip is now a piece anyone would be glad to have in the room.

# The finishing touch
photo: 20.jpg
caption: A free scatter cushion in a peacock print — the finishing flourish on every job.
Every re-cover leaves us with offcuts, and rather than waste good fabric we make a free scatter cushion to go with the chair. This one got a peacock print that picks up the teal and the blues perfectly. It is a small thing, but it is the kind of finish that makes a customer smile when they collect their chair.

--- FAQ ---

Q: How much does it cost to reupholster a Parker Knoll wing chair?
A: The labour on a straightforward Parker Knoll wing chair re-cover like this one is around five and a half hours, plus fabric and materials. The exact cost depends on the fabric you choose and whether the frame, webbing and foam can be reused — on this chair they could, which keeps it economical. Use our reupholstery cost estimator for a guide, and we are always happy to give a proper quote from a photograph.

Q: Are old Parker Knoll chairs worth reupholstering?
A: Almost always, yes. Parker Knoll built their frames from good hardwood and their post-war chairs used tensioned rubber webbing that lasts for decades — this one still had its original webbing with plenty of tension left. When the frame and the foam are sound, a re-cover gives you a better chair than most new ones, at a fraction of the cost of replacing it with the same quality.

Q: Can the original foam and webbing be reused?
A: On this chair, both were reused. The rubber webbing still had its tension, so it stayed. The foam had compressed over the years but was structurally fine, so rather than replace it we steamed it — which relaxes the compression and brings it back up to shape. A fresh layer of Dacron over the top gives the smooth, full finish. Reusing sound materials is better for the chair and better for your wallet.

Q: How long does it take to reupholster a wing chair?
A: This one took five and a half hours from start to finish — stripping the old cover with a staple unpicker, checking and steaming the foam, re-covering the wings, arms, back and seat in order, sewing the cushion and making the scatter cushion. A chair with frame damage or one needing full traditional re-webbing and stuffing would take longer, but a sound chair being re-covered in a plain fabric is a single day's work.
